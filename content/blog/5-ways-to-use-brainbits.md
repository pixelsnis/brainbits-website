**I've lost more good ideas than I care to admit.**

Not for lack of trying. I had Apple Notes, Notion, a brief and painful Obsidian phase. The problem was never having somewhere to put things — it was that every app made me stop and think before I could capture anything. Pick a folder, add a tag, give it a title. By the time I'd sorted all that out, the thought was already half-gone.

Brainbits is a note-taking app built around one idea: dump now, think later. You get thoughts in with zero friction, and when you need them later, you can actually find them. Here's what that looks like in practice.

---

## 1. Capture ideas before they disappear

[Screenshot of voice capture in Brainbits]

The best note-taking system is the one you'll actually use in the moment — walking, driving, mid-conversation. Brainbits lets you drop thoughts via text or voice, transcribes automatically, and saves instantly. No folders, no tags, no naming the note something sensible first.

Most of my best thinking happens when my hands are busy. Being able to just speak a thought and move on changed how much I actually hold onto.

---

## 2. Organize trip planning without the chaos

[Screenshot of a trip itinerary]

Travel research is naturally scattered — a restaurant rec from Reddit, a hotel tip from a friend, something saved from Instagram three weeks ago. Most people try to organize it as they go, which means either a mess of tabs or a Notes doc that quickly becomes impossible to navigate.

The better approach: drop each thing as a quick note whenever you find it. When you're ready to actually plan, Brainbits can pull everything you've captured into one structured, readable document — itinerary, packing list, reservations — without you having to do the assembly yourself.

---

## 3. Stay on top of multiple projects without a project management tool

[Screenshot of AI chat recalling project progress]

If you're freelancing or juggling several projects at once, the overhead of maintaining a proper project management system often costs more than it saves. What actually helps is being able to quickly answer "where did I leave off?" without reconstructing it from scratch every session.

Brainbits lets you jot progress notes as you go — messy is fine — and then ask in plain English when you need to pick back up. It searches across everything you've captured and gives you a direct answer, rather than making you scroll through pages of notes to find it.

---

## 4. Hold onto things that are worth remembering

[Screenshot of AI chat recalling museum visit notes]

Not every note is a task or a project. Sometimes it's a quote, an observation, something you found interesting that you'd like to be able to find again. Without a system, that stuff evaporates fast.

The fix is simple: drop it when it happens, in whatever words come naturally. Later, you can ask Brainbits for it in plain language — "what did I find interesting at that design exhibit?" — and it surfaces. The kind of recall that's supposed to happen naturally in your brain but usually doesn't.

---

## 5. Go from scattered thoughts to a shareable document

[Screenshot of generated document with project plan]

Trying to organize your thoughts while you're still having them kills momentum. The better workflow is to capture freely over days or weeks, then let Brainbits pull everything into a clean, structured document when you actually need one — a client pitch, a project plan, an essay outline.

Less staring at a blank page. More cleaning up something that already mostly exists.

---

## The bottom line

Most note-taking apps solve for storage. Brainbits solves for capture and recall — which is where most people actually lose things.

It's free to try for 7 days. If you're someone who thinks a lot and captures almost none of it, it's worth finding out what sticks around when you stop letting ideas evaporate.
